In This Text Set

This text set includes five informational texts, one of which is a timeline. All deal with communication throughout history.

Communication Through the Years is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign Communication Through the Years for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the table below to see where specific text features and text structures appear across the texts in this set.

Standards and Objectives

  • Science Standard

    Generate and compare multiple solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

  • Informational Text Focus

    In this set, students will read informational texts, including a timeline, to recognize how chronological structure and compare and contrast text structure—as well as text features such as captions, diagrams, photographs, and section headings—help their understanding as they read.

  • Content Objective

    Students will understand that advances in communication have occurred throughout history and have offered solutions to many problems.

  • Language Objective

    Students will describe and compare types of communication technology, recognizing the impact of improvements of communication tools and methods.

Big Question

How have changes to communication technology affected people’s lives?

Communication Through the Years

by Andrew McKean
Informational Text - Word Count: 551   Lexile: 860L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

  • Content Objective Students will learn about the sequence of events related to development of modes of communication.

  • Language Objective Students will identify events related to the development of modes of communication.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a timeline, a chronological text structure, can show the order of events.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how timelines show the order of important events.

Summary

This text provides a timeline that shows how modes of communication have changed throughout the decades, from the design of a telegraph system in 1837 to the widespread use of the smartphone today.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How have changes to communication technology affected people’s lives? Ask: How do you think Communication Through the Years will help you think about the Big Question? What is one type of technology that you use to communicate? How do you think it has changed people’s lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read timeline entries that have the words technology, telegraph, and devices. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: What is an example of technology? A device is something made for a specific purpose. Often, people use this word for electronic tools. What devices do you use? Long ago, people used telegraphs to send messages quickly. How do people send messages quickly today?

ORAL VOCABULARY
Some of the vocabulary in Communication Through the Years includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A network is a group of connected people or things. What do you think it means to have a network of computers?

T-Chart

Create a T-chart with a right column labeled Communication Invention and a left column labeled What It Helped People Do. Have students create their own columns in a notebook or on a piece of paper. Encourage them to use the charts to track inventions as they read, using vocabulary words from the text. An entry for telegraph might look like this:

[insert t-chart with right column called Communication and left column called What It Helped People Do; insert the following text in right column:] telegraph
[insert the following text in left column:] send messages quickly

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Communication Through the Years is a timeline, which means it is written with a chronological structure. Say: A text written with chronological structure tells the sequence of events in order. Let’s find out the order of two important events. Which came first, the telegraph or the first telephone system? Guide students to use the entries in the timeline to find that the telegraph was invented in 1837 and the first telephone system was invented in 1877.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about chronological structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand when events happened,” “helps readers understand the connections between events”

Text Features Ask: What are some of the features you notice in the timeline? Point out that each entry includes a year or range of years with a description. Say: Where could you look if you wanted to find out about events in the 1950s? Guide students to use the timeline to locate the descriptions. Then ask: Why do you think the writer decided to use a timeline instead of another type of text? What does it help you understand about the events?

Return to the T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the right column, write “Timeline.” Help students fill in the column with how a timeline helps them understand a writer’s ideas. For example: “shows the order of events,” “helps readers locate what happened in a year or time period”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Communication Through the Years, have them review their completed T-charts. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from Communication Through the Years to help students as they think about words such as technology, communicate, and devices.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among Communication Through the Years and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Communication Through the Years, you learned about the telegraph, a very early type of communication. What is another text you have read about early types of communication? What did you learn about communication technology?
  • Communication Through the Years explains how people communicate by using computers and other modern technology. What else have you read about modern methods for communication?
  • What is another text you have read that tells about improvements in the ways people communicate? What did you learn from it?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Changing World of Communication: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Communication Through the Years row with something they learned about changes in communication as time has passed.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How have changes to communication technology affected people’s lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • The timeline explains that a phone called “the brick” was an early cell phone. How have cell phones changed since then? How do people use cell phones today, and what do the phones help people do?
  • Which type of communication identified in the timeline did you find most interesting? Why? How did it change the way people communicate?

Write About The Big Question

Tell students: Think about the future. Suppose that the year is 2031. You have just invented a new way for people to communicate. Write an advertisement for this type of communication tool. Explain how it improves on earlier kinds of communication, and tell people why they should buy it. Create an illustration to go with your ad.

How Have Cell Phones Changed?

by Beth Kleinman
Informational Text - Word Count: 577   Lexile: 800L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize the many ways cell phones have changed and understand how new inventions help facilitate communication.

  • Language Objective Students will explain how cell phones work and how they have changed since the earliest cell phones.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure can show how cell phones of different time periods are alike and how they are different.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will recognize the ways in which photographs can help provide additional information and clarify ideas in a text.

Summary

This text compares and contrasts cell phones, from the earliest models to the smartphones of the present day. In addition, the text explains how a cell phone works.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How have changes to communication technology affected people’s lives? Ask: How do you think cell phones have affected people’s lives? How have cell phones changed over time?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words advances, signals, and connection. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: An advance is an event or invention that helps people do new things. How was the cell phone an advance in communication? A signal is something that sends information. What is an example of a signal? How do phones help people make connections with others?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in How Have Cell Phones Changed? includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: What predictions would you make about the design of cell phones in the future?

T-Charts

Demonstrate creating a T-chart with columns labeled Communication Invention and What It Helped People Do. Have students create their own T-charts in their notebooks or on sheets of paper. Ask them to track inventions in cellphone technology using the charts. An entry for smartphone might look like this:

[insert t-chart with right column called Communication Invention and left column called What It Helped People Do; insert the following text in right column:] smartphone
[insert the following text in left column:] make phone calls and use the Internet

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that How Have Cell Phones Changed? is written with a compare and contrast structure. Say: When writers use this structure, they show how things are alike and how they are different. Read screen 3 (Printable Text p. x) with students. Ask: How was the brick phone similar to today’s cell phones? How was it different?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare and contrast structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand how things are alike and different”

Text Features Say: Photographs are important features. They can provide new information or help the reader understand the information described in the text. Direct students’ attention to the photo of the brick phone. Ask: How does this photo help you understand the design and size of the brick phone?

Return to the T-chart. Write “Photographs” at the top of the right column. Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photographs help readers understand the text. For example: “help readers picture or see the writer’s ideas”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read How Have Cell Phones Changed?, have them review their completed T-charts. Ask: Are there any more words or phrases you would like to add?

Display these images from How Have Cell Phones Changed? to help students as they think about words such as connect, signal, and communicate.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among How Have Cell Phones Changed? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • You learned that cell phones changed because people’s needs and interests changed. What else have you read about changes in communication? Why did the changes happen?
  • In How Have Cell Phones Changed? you read that later cell phones included keypads with letters, so people could send texts on their phones. What have you read about inventions that helped people communicate in new ways?
  • In Communication Through the Years, you read about early telephone technology. How did this text add to your understanding of how phones have changed?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Changing World of Communication: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the How Have Cell Phones Changed? row with something they learned about ways cell phones make it possible to transfer information.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How have changes to communication technology affected people’s lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What can people do with a cell phone that they couldn’t do with other types of phones?
  • What is one important way that cell phones changed? How did it affect people’s lives?

Write About The Big Question

Say: Imagine that you are a time traveler. You have just traveled back in time to the year 1973, when the first cell phone was invented. How do you feel about your ability to communicate? What ability are you glad to have? What ability do you wish you had? Have students write a journal entry describing their feelings.

Television in the United States

adapted from myWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 589   Lexile: 880L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

  • Content Objective Students will understand how television developed and how it affected US culture.

  • Language Objective Students will explain how television developed and why it has had an impact on culture in the United States.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a chronological text structure can help readers understand the development of an invention.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will recognize how headings can help readers understand important information in a text.

Summary

This text explains the development of the television, as well as the impact of television on American culture.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: How have changes in communication technology affected people’s lives? Ask: How do you think Television in the United States will help you think about the Big Question? How does television help us communicate? How do you think it changed people’s lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words transmitting, technology, and broadcasts. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: How was the television an important technology for getting information to people? What would you expect to see on a news broadcast? When you transmit something, you send it. Does a television transmit information?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Television in the United States includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: How did televisions transmit information? What is another technology that transmits information?

T-Chart

Demonstrate creating a T-chart with columns labeled Communication Invention and What It Helped People Do. Have students create their own T-charts in their notebooks or on sheets of paper. Ask them to track inventions in television technology using the charts. An entry for Iconoscope might look like this:

[insert t-chart with right column called Communication Invention and left column called What It Helped People Do; insert the following text in right column:] Iconoscope
[insert the following text in left column:] send images from one place to another

During Reading

Text Structure Explain that Television in the United States is written with a chronological structure. Say: When writers use chronological structure, they write to show the time order of events. Direct students’ attention to screen 3 (Printable Text p. x). Have students find dates that help them understand the order in which events happened. Ask: Which earlier inventions helped lead to the invention of the TV?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about chronological structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand when events happened”

Text Features Direct students’ attention to screen 3 (Printable Text p. x). Say: This text includes headings, such as “How Did Television Begin?” How do headings help readers understand information in a text? Point out the next heading, on screen 5 (Printable Text p. x). Ask: What would you expect to read in this section?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with how headings help them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand what they are about to read”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Television in the United States, have them review their completed vocabulary T-charts. Ask: Are there any more words or phrases you would like to add?

Display these images from Television in the United States to help students as they think about words such as transmit and broadcast.

Encourage students to make connections among Television in the United States and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Television in the United States, you learned about changes in ways to send and receive images. What information have you read in other texts about sending and receiving information?
  • You read about effects the television has had on life in the United States. What else have you read about effects of a type of technology on everyday life?
  • In Communication Through the Years, you read about important inventions that helped people communicate. Do you think the television was as important for communication as the telephone? Why or why not?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Changing World of Communication: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Television in the United States row with something they learned about how television changed American life.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How have changes in communication technology affected people’s lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • How do you think televised debates changed politicians' campaigns and communications?
  • What are some important ways that television has changed since it was first invented? What effects did the changes have on people?

Write About the Big Question

Have students imagine they are news reporters in 1960, about to introduce two candidates running for president of the United States. The two are about to have a debate. It will be the first presidential debate shown on television. Ask students to write an introduction for the debate, explaining why it is important that the debate will be shown on television.

Codes Through History

by Andi Patel
Informational Text - Word Count: 572   Lexile: 880L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

  • Content Objective Students will recognize the purposes of codes and how codes have developed over time.

  • Language Objective Students will explain how codes have helped people communicate.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure can show how codes are alike and different.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will recognize the value of headings in showing the type of information in a specific section of a text.

Summary

This text compares and contrasts a variety of types of codes. It shows the important role these codes have played—and continue to play—in communication.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: How have changes in communication technology affected people’s lives? Ask: How do you think Codes Through History will help you think about the Big Question? What do you already know about codes and their importance in communication? How do you think codes might have changed over time?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words symbols, signal, and system. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Draw a smiley face and ask: Is this a symbol? What does this symbol mean? How would you describe a system of communication? A signal can be a message or an alert. What kind of code would you use to send a signal?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Codes Through History includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A code is a system of numbers, letters, or other symbols. What are some examples of codes? How might you make your own code?

T-Chart

Demonstrate creating a T-chart with columns labeled Communication Invention and What It Helped People Do. Have students create their own T-charts in their notebooks or on sheets of paper. Ask them to add details about codes in history using the charts. An entry for semaphore code might look like this:

[insert t-chart with right column called Communication Invention and left column called What It Helped People Do; insert the following text in right column:] semaphore code
[insert the following text in left column:] send messages to people at sea

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Codes Through History is written with a compare and contrast structure. Say: Writers compare by showing how two or more things are the same. They contrast by showing how the things are different. Point out the pictures of semaphore code and Morse code. Say: These are both codes that help people send messages. How do you think these two codes are different?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare and contrast structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand when events happened”

Text Features Say: You’ve read many texts that have phrases or words in bold print above a group of words. These are section headings. They tell you something about the information you are about to read in a specific section of a text. Point out the bold heading What Is a Code? Ask: What does this heading tell you about the information in this section? Guide students to understand that the heading tells them they will read to learn what a code is, which will prepare them to read the rest of the text.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with how headings help them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand what they are about to read”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Codes Through History, have them review their completed T-charts. Ask: Are there any more words or phrases you would like to add?

Display these images from Codes Through History to help students as they think about words such as symbols, signal, and system.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among Codes Through History and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Codes Through History, you learned that a telegraph could send messages across great distances. What information have you read in other texts about sending messages across distances?
  • Codes Through History describes using a password, a type of code people use for their computers. What have you read about computers in other texts?
  • Think back to Communication Through the Years. What did you read about the telegraph in that text? How did Codes Through History add to your understanding about the importance of this technology?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Changing World of Communication: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Codes Through History row with something they learned about how codes help people communicate.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How have changes in communication technology affected people’s lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Why is semaphore code used less often now than it was during the 1800s?
  • What were some of the reasons people wanted to develop new codes? How did the codes help them communicate with others?

Write About the Big Question

Tell students to work with a partner. Say: Each of you will write a short message to the other. Then, before showing the message to your partner, you will create a code for each letter you use. For example, a circle might represent the letter A, and a square might represent the letter B. You could also use images such as a hat to represent the letter A and a glove to represent the letter B. Be creative. Choose the symbols you want to use for your message. Then write the message in code. Give your message to your partner. Each of you will try to crack the other’s code. After 10 minutes, you will share your symbols and the letter each symbol represents. Then your partner will write the message in letters to create words and read the entire message.

The History of the Internet

by Stephen Pasgale
Informational Text - Word Count: 554   Lexile: 860L

Instructional Focus

  • Science Standard Generate and compare multiple solutions that use patterns to transfer information.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the historical events that led to the development of the Internet, as well as the connection between the Internet and the World Wide Web.

  • Language Objective Students will explain historical events and the role they played in the development of the Internet.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a chronological text structure can show how events in history helped lead to changes in communication.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how a map can help them locate and understand information.

Summary

This text explains how US military and government researchers developed computer networks that led to the Internet. Eventually, the Internet became a tool available to all, with the World Wide Web making use of the Internet possible.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: How have changes in communication technology affected people’s lives? Ask: How do people use the Internet today? How do you think they communicated before the Internet? How did the Internet change people’s lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words network, Internet, and device. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: A network is a group of connected people or things. What does it mean for a computer to be part of a network? What kinds of devices can connect to the Internet?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in The History of the Internet includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A system is a group of parts that work together. Minitel is one example of a system. What is another example?

T-Chart

Create a T-chart with a right column labeled Communication Invention and a left column labeled What It Helped People Do. Have students create their own columns in a notebook or on a piece of paper. Encourage them to use the charts to track inventions as they read, using vocabulary words from the text. An entry for ARPANET might look like this:

[insert t-chart with right column called Communication Invention and left column called What It Helped People Do; insert the following text in right column:] ARPANET
[insert the following text in left column:] connect computers in a network

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that The History of the Internet is written with a chronological structure. Say: A text written with this structure tells the time order of events, beginning with the earliest event related to the topic. Direct students’ attention to the section of the text titled Why It All Began. Ask a student to summarize the section. You may wish to use a timeline to track the events in the text, with entries such as the following: 1950s: Researchers needed to share information; 1958: ARPA was created; 1980s: The Internet as we know it today began. Ask: Why do you think the writer chose to describe these events in the order they happened?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about chronological structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “helps readers understand the connection between events”

Text Features Direct students’ attention to the map on screen 5 (Printable Text p. x) and explain that it shows ARPANET, a network of computers that could share information with each other. Say: Maps are useful features that help readers understand where things are located. Point out a dot on the map and tell students that it represents one computer in the network. Ask: What does the map help you understand about ARPANET? (Possible responses: how many computers were part of the network; where computers in ARPANET were located)

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Maps.” Help students fill in the column with how maps help them understand a writer’s ideas. For example: “give information about where something is located”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read The History of the Internet, have them review their completed T-charts. Ask: Are there any more words you would like to add?

Display these images from The History of the Internet to help students as they think about words such as network, Internet, and communication.

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections among The History of the Internet and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In The History of the Internet, you learned about reasons people wanted to use a tool like the Internet. What else have you read about people’s reasons for creating new communication tools?
  • The History of the Internet describes the way people communicated before the Internet was available. What else have you read about earlier communications technology in other texts?
  • Think back to Communication Through the Years. What did you learn about the Internet from that text? What else do you know about it now that you have read The History of the Internet?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Changing World of Communication: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in The History of the Internet row with something they learned about sharing information using the Internet and World Wide Web.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion related to this text’s Big Question: How have changes in communication technology affected people's lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • President Eisenhower formed ARPA. How did President Eisenhower’s decision to form ARPA lead to easier communication today?
  • What do you think the future holds for new methods of communication? Will the Internet still be a major way for people to communicate?

Write About the Big Question

Tell students: Imagine that the year is 1980. You are a government official who uses ARPANET, but you realize that the ARPANET technology could be valuable to everyone. Write a persuasive speech to tell why ARPANET should be available to all people. Invite students to share their speeches, if they wish.

Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round Up

Example Sentences Have students review their T-charts with vocabulary related to inventions in communication. Ask them to choose one word and write a sentence that shows their understanding of the word’s meaning, or say a sentence with the word to a partner. Ask them to repeat the activity for three more words. If time permits, you might ask students to write a paragraph that includes all four words and shows how they are related.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion related to the Big Question: How have changes to communication technology affected people’s lives? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their T-charts webs as they can.

  • What did you already know about methods of communication throughout history before you began reading this text set?
  • You’ve read many texts about changes in tools and methods of communication throughout the years. What inventions did you learn about for the first time?
  • What would you still like to learn about communication tools and methods of communication?
  • Tell about some texts you’ve read that could be most useful in helping you answer the Big Question.

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: Communication Invention

Tell students they will create a plan and diagram for an invention involving a method of communication. Provide the following instructions:

  • The invention should use one of the communication methods you have read about.
  • The invention should help improve this communication method.
  • In your diagram, show the appearance of the invention and the way it works. Include labels.
  • Write at least two paragraphs. In your paragraphs, do the following:
  • Explain the reason for your invention.
  • Tell how your invention works.
  • Identify the communication method that gave you the ideas for your invention.

Students can refer to information they have written in Write About the Big Question for each text to aid their thinking and planning. They may also use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their projects. Encourage students to use the vocabulary they’ve learned from the texts.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
